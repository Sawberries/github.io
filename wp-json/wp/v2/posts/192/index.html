In the mid-20th century, a bold, angular architectural style emerged as a celebration of post-war renewal, innovation, and symbolic strength. Brutalism<\/a>, known for its bare, monochrome, industrial materials like concrete, brick, and steel, became a way for centers of influence like municipal hubs, government buildings, and cultural institutions to convey magnificent resilience and contemporaneity. Religious architecture was no exception.<\/p>\n

There is hardly a more symbolic building than a church or cathedral, from the pilgrimage-like progression down the nave toward the altar to the lofty height and sweeping arches that draw the eye upward as a metaphorical connection to Heaven. And starting the 1950s, architects began designing cavernous brutalist buildings to house congregations around Europe, taking ecclesiastical structures in remarkable new directions.<\/p>\n

In his new book Sacred Modernity, <\/em>photographer Jamie McGregor Smith<\/a> explores Europe\u2019s most stunning brutalist churches, capturing cavernous meeting halls, remarkable geometry, and characteristic concrete and brick textures. Published by Hatje Cantz<\/a>, the volume traces the dramatic, modernizing shift in the Catholic church in the early 1960s, marked by the Vatican\u2019s search for an appropriate architectural language to show that the Catholic Church was still relevant in contemporary society.<\/p>\n

\u00a0<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

St. Paulus Kirche, Weckhoven, Dussoldorf, Germany. Designed by Fritz Schaller and Stefan Polo\u0301nyi, constructed 1966-1970<\/p>\n<\/div>\n

Smith has long been captivated by industrial and urban design. Inspired by the New Topographics<\/a> documentary photography movement in the U.S., he began focusing on the defunct pottery industry in the British Midlands and iron ore processing plants in Middlesbrough. When he moved from London to Vienna in the summer of 2018, he was immediately struck by a seeming paradox when he visited a impressively blocky, asymmetrical church in the district of Liesing. He says:<\/p>\n

\n

The concept for the church began life as a sculpture, the artist believing its design had been delivered to him by God in a dream. I was bewildered that this piece of progressive art, consisting of 152 irregular concrete blocks, had been commissioned by such a conservative institution. It redefined my idea of what a church could be: at once beautiful yet brutal.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n

Smith broadened his search and quickly became enthralled by the forward-thinking movement in sacred design, spurring a series that aims to collate the religious architecture of mid-century high modernity. At more than 200 pages with well over 100 photographs, the new volume catalogues locations across the continent, highlighting the distinctive use of cast concrete, light-catching facets, and monumental proportions.<\/p>\n

Sacred Modernity <\/em>is scheduled for release in the U.S. on My 14, and you can peorder a copy now on Bookshop<\/a>. Find more on the artist\u2019s website<\/a> and Instagram<\/a>.<\/p>\n
